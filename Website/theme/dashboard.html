RhoBot Pump And Dump Predictor

Can ML predictions help prevent crypto-fraud?

RhoBot.io is a classification-based machine-learning prediction tool. It is born out of a desire to cleanse small crypto-tokens of fraudulent ‘pump-and-dump’s. These are events where an anonymous influencer chooses a small cap coin, and announces to their followers a ‘signal’ to rapidly purchase that coin. The coins price violently surges, and the organisers, who purchased beforehand, sell their tokens onto members, causing many to unwillingly lose their funds. The CFTC have attempted to prevent these fraudulent events by urging whistle-blowers for information – but given the anonymous nature of blockchains and encrypted chatrooms, have not covered ground. I thus approach this issue from another perspective: that the development of a free prediction tool that enables the followers of these groups to predict and pre-purchase the coins, would prevent any price-spike, and therefore remove the monetary incentive from the organising influencers.

Methodology

Predictions of which coins will pump are calculated every 15 minutes, and are available 5 minutes before each quater-hour. For example, the prediction for 16:45 will be available at 16:40. The algorithm is consistently updated.

The ROC curve

The Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve describes how both the false positive and true positive rate adjusts when the threshold of the classifier changes. A good model would have have both a very low false-positive rate and a high true positive rate.

RhoBot has room for improvement - and as more pump observations are gathered, it will increase in specificity.

A Random Forest Tree

Random Forests work by taking the dataset, repeatedly sampling it with with duplicates, then creating decision trees for each new set. When predicting each tree is given observation and votes it true or false - the majority vote is the decision.

This is the first tree from the Random Forest. The current version has 34 individual trees, each with a different shape.

The Confusion Matrix

This confusion matrix describes the performace of the classifier. As is apparent, it has an accuracy of 92.3%, misclassification of 7.62%, precision of 37.5%, recall of 20.6% and an f1 score of 0.265.

Any changes to the classification threshold trigger an inversely proportion tradeoff between recall and precision. Thus, the best metric for judgement is the f1 score.

Feature Selection

This is a supervised machine learning model - so feature selection is paramount to a precision. To understand what forms a desision for a chosen coin, I reached out to some of the organisers. Expectedly, they informed me that the 3 largest factors were market-cap, volume and volatility in returns. Many wanted a coin that was extremely illiquid as it would prevent trading bots from instantly arbitraging the coin.

For what Random Forests lack in paramater intuition, they make up for in their extremely intuitive description of feature importance. They present each features importance as a percentage - measuring how much the feature contributes to the trees total gini impurity reduction.

The most important features of the random forrest are listed below:
